A shared place

Respect Vallecito

One of the last, best mountain-lake valleys in Colorado — here's how we all keep it that way. We live here, and we're glad you're here.

What you're standing in

A pretty extraordinary corner of Colorado

Before the "how," the "why." A few things about this valley that are worth knowing — and worth protecting. Around 400–500 of us live here year-round (about 2,000 in summer), surrounded by more than 2.5 million acres of public land. (Visit Durango)

⛰️ Colorado's largest Wilderness is the backyard

The Weminuche wraps the valley: 499,771 acres — the Forest Service calls it "three-quarters the size of Rhode Island" — with 50 miles of the Continental Divide and three 14ers (Eolus, Sunlight, Windom). Its snowmelt feeds both the Rio Grande and the San Juan.

San Juan NF · wilderness.net

🥾 Two great gateways start right here

From the lake, the Vallecito Creek Trail (#529) climbs roughly 18 miles to the Divide, and the Pine River Trail (#523) reaches what the Forest Service calls "some of the finest fly-fishing anywhere."

Trail guide · San Juan NF

💧 The water carries an 1868 priority date

This reservoir honors some of the oldest water rights in the basin. The Southern Ute Indian Tribe owns a one-sixth interest in Vallecito Reservoir, confirmed in a 1988 federal settlement. The wilderness above it is named for the Weminuche band of the Ute people, whose homeland this is; "Vallecito" is Spanish for "little valley."

PRID — Pine River Project history

🏗️ A New Deal lake

The Bureau of Reclamation built Vallecito Dam between 1938 and 1941. WPA workers and CCC enrollees cleared some five million board feet of timber from the valley floor before the water rose — ranchers, farmers, and Utes had pushed for it together.

PRID — Pine River Project history

🐏 The bighorns above the creek are originals

The sheep on the slopes above Vallecito Creek are a native, genetically distinct herd that never died out — among only a few hundred animals across three herds in these mountains. Worth a long look through binoculars, and a wide berth.

Mountain Studies Institute

🐾 The cats came back

218 Canada lynx were released into the San Juans between 1999 and 2007 — Colorado's most successful carnivore reintroduction. Moose, reintroduced from 1991, now wander the high drainages too.

Colorado Parks & Wildlife

🦅 Osprey overhead, eagles on the shore

The Colorado Birding Trail calls osprey "plentiful" here; bald eagles cruise the shoreline, and loons and goldeneye pass through on migration. Bring the binoculars.

Colorado Birding Trail

🎣 A record-setting fishery

Vallecito has produced a state-record northern pike, gives up trophy pike regularly, and grows brown trout to 30 inches. A genuinely special place to fish — let's keep it that way.

CPW — Vallecito Reservoir · our fishing guide

How we keep it this way

A handful of shared standards

None of this is hard — it's just how a small community keeps a wild place wild. Most of it is also the law, so each one links to the actual rule if you want it. Here's how we all do it.

🚗 Slow down on the county roads

CR 501 and CR 500 are mountain county roads shared with kids, dogs, deer, and cyclists, and the Sheriff patrols the lake (your PRID recreation fees help pay for it). Obey the posted limits — they're slower than you'd think, for good reasons you'll see around the next bend.

PRID

🏍️ Ride legal — trails, not roads

In Colorado this is state law, not local grumpiness: OHVs and side-by-sides can't travel a public road unless the county has designated it open — and no La Plata County road has been designated open. Crossings only. The legal (and better) riding is on San Juan National Forest routes shown on the Motor Vehicle Use Map, with a CPW OHV registration ($26.25).

Kids: ages 10–15 may ride designated trails only under the direct, visual supervision of a licensed driver; 16+ can ride on their own — never on the roads. CPW and its education partners direct riders under 18 to wear a DOT helmet.

Middle Mountain ride guide → · C.R.S. 33-14.5-108 · Stay The Trail

🗑️ Pack it out

The marina, the market, and the campgrounds all have dumpsters, so litter is one problem we can always solve together. (For the record, Colorado littering carries fines starting at $20, and it's prohibited on National Forest land too — but mostly it just doesn't belong here.)

C.R.S. 18-4-511 · 36 CFR 261.11

🔇 Sound carries on the water

A voice or a stereo travels a surprising distance across a still lake. Forest Service campground quiet hours run 10 p.m.–6 a.m. (easy on the generators), and Colorado's noise statute sets nighttime limits. The loons and the neighbors both thank you.

San Juan NF — Vallecito CG · C.R.S. 25-12-103

🔥 Fire is the one mistake this valley can't take back

Missionary Ridge burned more than 70,000 acres above this lake in 2002 — everyone here remembers. Fireworks that leave the ground are always illegal in Colorado, and all fireworks are banned on National Forest land year-round. Always check the current fire-restriction status before any flame, campfire, or stove.

Live fire restrictions → · CO DFPC · 36 CFR 261.16

🦌 Don't feed the wildlife — secure the trash

A fed bear is a dead bear. Feeding big game is illegal in Colorado, and La Plata County asks everyone at the lake to keep trash bear-secure. Lock it up, store it inside, and enjoy the animals wild — which is the whole point of seeing them here.

Our wildlife guide → · CPW — living with wildlife

🚁 Leave the drones out of the Wilderness

Drones can't launch, land, or operate from designated Wilderness — the Weminuche included. Over the lake and the cabins, follow FAA rules and a little courtesy; a quiet sky is part of what people drive all this way for.

36 CFR 261.18 · FAA UAS rules

🚤 On the water

Colorado boating rules apply — life jackets aboard, careful operation, working mufflers — and every trailered boat is inspected for aquatic nuisance species at the ramp, so arrive clean, drained, and dry. PRID manages recreation on the reservoir.

CPW boating · PRID · permits

🏡 If you host guests

Unincorporated La Plata County doesn't require a short-term-rental permit, but it does require lodging-tax collection — and it publishes a short Good Neighbor Policy covering noise, parking, trash, pets, and fire. Print it and leave it on the fridge for your guests; it's one of the kindest things you can do for your neighbors.

La Plata County — Good Neighbor Policy · VRBO info

And this isn't a visitors-only list. Those of us here year-round live by the same trash-and-bear, noise, burn-restriction, and trail rules — we're all looking after the same place.

Why it's worth it

This valley has come back before

When the Missionary Ridge Fire swept the ridgelines in 2002, the community answered with art: the Tour of Carvings — fourteen sculptures cut from fire-killed trees, each crowned with a carved flame. In October 2025, when Vallecito Creek rose to a record flood and around 350 homes evacuated, the valley began rebuilding again, the way it always has.

This place has come back from fire and from flood twice in a generation. What we all do on an ordinary Tuesday — slow down, pack it out, lock the trash, skip the fireworks, ride the right trails — is how it stays extraordinary.

Welcome to Vallecito. We're genuinely glad you're here.
