Sharing the valley

Wildlife & you

We share this valley with bears, moose, eagles, and elk — a few small habits keep them wild and everyone safe. Here's how, and who to call.

Why it matters here

Three things worth knowing

🐻 This county leads the state

In 2024, La Plata County accounted for 14% of all bears euthanized and 25% of all bear relocations in Colorado — more than any other county. Almost all of it traces back to food and trash.

Bear Smart Durango, 2024 recap

🫎 Moose, not bears, are the danger

CPW calls moose "one of the more dangerous animals we have… not scared of anything" — and unleashed dogs are by far the most common trigger for moose attacks in Colorado. They've moved into the Weminuche above the lake.

Colorado Parks & Wildlife

🍫 "A candy bar is all it takes"

A bear smells food up to five miles off and, in fall, eats around the clock for ~20,000 calories a day. Bear Smart's line says it best: a candy bar or an empty soda can left in your car is all it takes to total it.

Bear Smart Durango

🐻 Who to trust locally: Bear Smart Durango

Bear Smart Durango is a local nonprofit (founded 2003, director Bryan Peterson) working to reduce human–bear conflict across the Durango and La Plata County area — loaner bear-resistant cans, electric-fence incentives, fruit-gleaning, bird-feeder exchanges, education, and the policy work behind the city and county bear-trash rules. They helped launch the county bear hotline, and offer a $100 rebate toward a bear-resistant container.

Who you'll meet

One habit per neighbor

Each animal really comes down to one change in what we do. That's the whole list.

🐻 Black bears — lock up every scent

Trash out only the morning of pickup (never overnight); nothing scented in cars or tents; pull bird feeders while bears are out (a 7-lb seed tube is 12,000+ calories); pick fruit early, clean grills, feed pets indoors. If you meet one: don't run or climb — stand your ground, yell, throw things; bear spray at ~40 ft. If a black bear actually attacks, fight back — do not play dead (that's black bears, not grizzlies). CPW runs a two-strike policy, so a food-conditioned bear often ends up dead. A fed bear is a dead bear.

CPW — living with bears · Bear Smart

🫎 Moose — leash the dog, give wide berth

The most dangerous large animal here. Dogs read as wolves and trigger charges, so keep yours leashed and close. Give extra room to cows with calves and to anything in the willows, and back away if a moose lays its ears back or raises its hackles.

CPW — living with moose

🦁 Mountain lions (rare) — never run

Sightings are uncommon. If you do meet one: speak calmly, back away, look big — raise your arms or open your jacket — pick up kids and pets, and throw things if it approaches. Never run, and never approach a lion that's feeding or with kittens.

CPW — living with lions

🦌 Elk & deer — slow down at dawn & dusk

Most vehicle collisions happen at first and last light, especially during spring and fall migration along CR 501 — so ease off then. And never touch a fawn or calf in spring; it isn't abandoned, the mother is nearby.

CPW — wildlife collisions

🦅 Osprey & eagles — keep your distance from nests

Osprey nest right at the lake and bald eagles work the shoreline; both are federally protected. Give nests a wide berth (Colorado raptor guidance is roughly ⅛ mile from osprey nests, ½ mile from active eagle nests). Using non-lead tackle is a great way to protect eagles and loons — a best practice, not a local rule.

CPW — raptors

🐍 Rattlesnakes — a lower-elevation concern

At about 7,700 ft the lake is near their upper limit — they're far more likely on warm canyon hikes down toward Bayfield and the lower Pine River than at the shoreline. Watch your step and your hands on those lower, sunnier trails; no need to worry at the water's edge.

Colorado Parks & Wildlife

🦝 Camp scavengers — secure food & coolers

Raccoons, skunks, and ground squirrels work every campground. The fix is the same as for bears: food and coolers locked in a hard-sided vehicle or a bear box, nothing scented in the tent, scraps packed out.

CPW — protect your campsite

🐄 Open-range cattle — slow down, pass calmly

The San Juan National Forest is actively grazed, so expect cattle on the forest roads through summer (including the Pine River area). Slow down, give them room, pass calmly, and don't haze them — it's a courtesy and a safety thing both.

San Juan National Forest

📣 See a problem? Report it
  • 🐻 La Plata County Bear Hotline — (970) 247-BEAR (2327) (bear-in-trash, conflict, sightings)
  • Colorado Parks & Wildlife — Durango office — (970) 247-0855
  • After hours / any emergency — 911 (or Colorado State Patrol for a road-side animal hazard)

Reporting early — before a bear gets a food reward — is what actually keeps it alive. See all emergency & lake contacts, and the feeding-wildlife law on Respect Vallecito.

If you rent your place

Hosts: set your guests up to be bear smart

Vacation guests don't know the local rules until someone tells them — and one careless week can mean a dead bear next door. Bear Smart Durango's guidance for rentals:

  • Provide bear-resistant cans and ask guests to keep the lids locked; trash out only the morning of pickup.
  • Doors, windows, garage, and vehicles closed and locked; nothing scented left in cars.
  • Pets fed indoors; grills cleaned after use; report any bear activity to the hotline.
  • Print Bear Smart's visitor flyer and leave it for your guests — pair it with the county Good Neighbor Policy.
