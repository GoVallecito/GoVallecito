Living in Vallecito · Buying Property Near the Lake

Buying property near the lake

Mountain-lake property has its own checklist. Here is what to look at before you buy and how the process works up here — with a local broker who lives it.

Buy the water & the road first

Wells & water

Water is the first thing to check, not the view. In Colorado, a parcel under 35 acres generally qualifies only for a household-use-only well permit — indoor water for the home, but no lawn irrigation and no livestock. At 35 acres or more you can get a domestic well (up to three dwellings and an acre of irrigation). So check the permit class before you fall for the pasture. Cisterns and hauled water are a real option up here (there's a bulk fill station in the district). And note the state doesn't test private wells — that's on you (CDPHE). Sources: CO Division of Water Resources Guideline 2023-1; CSU Extension 6.700.

🏡 Old-timer's rule of thumb

Buy the road and the water before you buy the view — and visit the lot in February before you close in June. Snow tells you the truth about access.

The other big checks

Septic, access & insurance

Septic: most lake homes are on septic, and Colorado requires a third-party inspection at sale that produces a La Plata County Public Health acceptance document ($110, valid 12 months); systems finaled within the last four years are generally exempt. Confirm the current program wording with the county at closing. Source: La Plata County Public Health septic program.

Access: the county maintains 653 miles of road but never private roads (and limits taking on new ones). If the property is on a private road, demand the recorded road-maintenance agreement and find out who plows — winter access is a real cost line, not a footnote.

Insurance: bind early. Wildland-urban-interface underwriting is slow and selective; the Colorado FAIR Plan is the floor, not the goal, and HB25-1182 means documented mitigation now earns credit. Do your defensible-space homework before you shop coverage.

Money & paper

Taxes, title & renting it out

Property taxes are pleasantly low — Colorado sets a residential assessment rate in the rough range of 6–7% by statute (SB24-233); check current figures with the La Plata County Assessor rather than any single quoted number.

Title: the reservoir itself sits on federal Bureau of Reclamation project land, so it's worth having your title company confirm whether a given lot is fee-simple ground in your work — a simple question to settle in writing.

Short-term rental? Unincorporated La Plata County requires lodging-tax collection (no STR permit) and publishes a Good Neighbor Policy — see Respect Vallecito. For listings and local market guidance, Julie Coffelt is the site's real-estate partner.

🪵 Old-timer's rule of thumb

Read the well permit, the septic acceptance, and the road agreement before you read the listing again. The view sells itself; those three papers are what you actually live with.
