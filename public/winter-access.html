Living in Vallecito · Winter & Access

Winter & getting in and out

CR 501 climbs, and it ices over before US-160 does. Here's what winter access really looks like up here — and the old habits that make it easy instead of stressful.

The plow

CR 501 is a Level 1 priority road

This surprises people: the county plows CR 501 as a Level 1 road — "continuous all-weather access," cleared before the school buses run. CR 500, 501A, and 502 are Level 2 (same-day target, first to slip in a big dump), and gravel roads get plowed after about 4 inches. The District V (Bayfield) road shop is (970) 382-6420. Source: La Plata County snow-removal priorities.

Locals will tell you the plows are the lifeline up here, and the community backs them 100%. Still — Level 1 means maintained, not bare and dry. The road ices on the shady pitches and the switchbacks well before the highway does, so you drive it like the mountain road it is.

🪵 Old-timer's rule of thumb

A shovel, a blanket, and a headlamp live in the truck from Halloween to Easter — and you keep the gas tank above half all winter. The storm you get stuck in is never the one you planned for.

How much snow, really

About 85 inches a year at the dam

You'll hear big numbers thrown around. The honest figure at lake elevation is roughly 85 inches a year at the dam (NOAA 1991–2020 normals) — about double what Durango gets, but nowhere near the 200-plus inches people quote. Those belong to the high country above the lake (the Weminuche SNOTEL sites), not the shoreline. Source: NOAA/NCEI climate normals, station USC00058582.

It's real winter — cold, clear, and snowy — but it's a high-desert mountain winter: storms come through and then the sun comes back. Check live conditions and road status before you head down the hill.

Driving the storm

The traction law (and where it applies)

Colorado's traction law covers the state highways — US 160 and US 550 — not CR 501. When it's active you need all-wheel/four-wheel drive with at least 3/16-inch winter-rated tread, or chains / an approved traction device. Fines run roughly $50–$130, and $650-plus if you spin out and block the road (CDOT's own pages vary on the exact numbers). Source: CDOT traction law.

Up on CR 501 there's no statute — just physics. Good winter tires aren't optional for full-time life here, and the smart move is to time errands around the storm rather than through it: most systems clear within a day, and the county is plowing the whole time.

🏔️ Old-timer's rule of thumb

Run the errands Monday through Thursday and watch the sky. Town will still be there after the storm; the icy drive home in the dark is the part you can skip.

Power & heat

Outages, wood heat, and burn permits

Power is La Plata Electric Association, a member co-op; their live outage map is worth a bookmark. Winter outages do happen north of Bayfield, so most full-timers keep a backup — a generator, or a wood or pellet stove. New stoves must be EPA-2015-compliant, but there's no routine indoor-burning ban here.

Outdoor burning needs a free one-year burn permit from Upper Pine River Fire — issued seven days a week, including at Station 4 (North Vallecito), (970) 444-0030. Permits automatically suspend on Red Flag days, so always check the current fire status first. More in our Firewise guide. Source: upperpinefpd.org.

Stay in the loop

Sign up for LPC Alerts!

La Plata County's emergency notification system is LPC Alerts! — sign up at lpcgov.org/LPCAlerts. Save the numbers it calls from — (970) 385-8700 (calls) and 58339 (texts) — so a 2 a.m. evacuation or road-closure message is one you actually answer. It carries wildfire, flood, and winter-emergency notices.

🪵 Old-timer's rule of thumb

The woodshed's full by Labor Day, the propane's topped off before the first storm, and you know your nearest neighbor's number by heart. Up here, the neighbor who plows you out beats any app.
