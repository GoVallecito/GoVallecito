Launching your boat at Vallecito Lake

A first-timer's guide to getting your own boat on the water here — the paperwork you need, the inspection everyone goes through, how the ramp works, and the mountain-lake tricks that take most people a season or two to learn. Read it once and you'll launch like a local.

Start here

The 60-second version

Before your trailer ever reaches the water you need three things: a registered boat, a Colorado ANS stamp (motorboats and sailboats), and a PRID recreation permit. At the lake, every trailered or motorized boat passes a quick, free aquatic-nuisance-species inspection at the station by the marina — it's the law. Show up clean, drained, and dry, have your paperwork handy, launch early on summer weekends, and keep an eye on the afternoon weather. The rest of this guide fills in the details.

Before you tow in

The paperwork

🚤 Boat registration

Colorado-registered motorboats and sailboats must display current registration numbers and validation decals. Visiting from out of state? Your home registration generally covers a short trip — check CPW for non-resident rules.

🐚 ANS stamp

Every motorboat and sailboat needs an Aquatic Nuisance Species stamp before launching — $25 resident, $50 non-resident. Buy it at cpwshop.com or any CPW office, and keep your receipt (printed or on your phone) with you on the water.

🎫 PRID permit

A Pine River Irrigation District recreation permit is required to use the lake: $6/day per vehicle or $60/year. Motorized or trailered boats add a $12/day combo. See Licenses & permits for where to buy.

Non-motorized kayaks, canoes, and SUPs don't need an ANS stamp or the boat combo — just the per-vehicle recreation permit.

At the lake

The inspection — what to expect

Colorado law requires every trailered or motorized watercraft to pass an ANS inspection before it enters the water. At Vallecito Reservoir the station is right by the marina, and inspectors are on-site daily through the season. It's free and usually takes just a few minutes if you arrive prepared.

The whole system runs on three words — Clean, Drain, Dry:

  • Clean: no mud, plants, or debris on the hull, trailer, or motor.
  • Drain: Colorado law says you must pull the drain plug and empty all water — bilge, livewells, ballast — before you transport. Arrive at the ramp with the plug out.
  • Dry: the drier the boat, the faster the inspection. If you've recently boated in mussel-positive water elsewhere, expect a decontamination step.

One easy-to-forget step: put the drain plug back in after inspection and before you back down the ramp. It's the most common rookie mistake on any lake.

Launch day

Step by step at the ramp

Vallecito has one public boat ramp, managed by PRID and open May 1 – October 31, roughly 6 a.m. – 8 p.m. (a little shorter early and late in the season). One ramp means courtesy and speed matter — do your prep before you take your turn.

  1. Prep in the staging area, not on the ramp. Remove tie-down straps, load your gear, attach the bow line and a fender, raise the motor, and reinstall the drain plug.
  2. Back down slowly with a spotter. Hands at the bottom of the wheel, small corrections. Don't dunk the trailer hubs deeper than you need to.
  3. Float it off, walk the boat to the courtesy dock on its line, and move your truck and trailer to parking right away.
  4. Retrieving: stage the trailer, line the boat up, winch it snug, then pull forward and clear the ramp before you strap down and unload.

Always have your registration and ANS proof aboard, and clip the engine cut-off (kill switch) lanyard before you start.

Mountain-lake know-how

What's different up here

Vallecito sits at nearly 7,700 feet — higher than almost any lake most visitors have launched on. A few things behave differently than they do at lower elevation:

🏔️ Thin air, less power

Engines make noticeably less power at altitude and props feel sluggish. Allow extra room to get on plane, don't overload a pontoon, and if you run a carbureted motor it may need altitude jetting.

⛈️ Afternoon weather

Mountain thunderstorms and canyon winds build fast on summer afternoons. Launch early, watch the sky, and head in when clouds stack up — lightning and whitecaps arrive quickly. Check live conditions first.

🥶 Cold water

This is snowmelt. Early-season water is cold enough to cause trouble fast if someone goes in — dress for the water, not the air, and keep life jackets on.

📉 Changing lake level

Vallecito is an irrigation reservoir, so the level drops through summer. Watch for newly shallow spots, stumps, and rocks late in the season, and check the current lake level before you explore the shallows or the upper end.

On the water

Rules & courtesy

  • Life jackets: one U.S. Coast Guard-approved PFD for every person aboard, and children 12 and under must wear one any time the boat is underway. Boats 16 feet and longer also need a throwable (Type IV).
  • Who can drive: Colorado requires motorboat operators to be 18, or 14–17 with a state-approved boating safety course. (These rules changed recently — confirm with CPW.)
  • No-wake zones: keep to wakeless speed near the marina, docks, the swim area, anchored boats, and close to shore — it's both the rule and good manners.
  • Carry the basics: a fire extinguisher, a sound device (whistle or horn), and navigation lights if you'll be out near dawn, dusk, or after dark.
  • Don't drink and drive a boat. Boating under the influence carries the same kind of penalties as a DUI.

For the full, current regulations, see Colorado Parks & Wildlife boating safety.

Local tips

From people who launch here

  • Arrive early on weekends and holidays. Parking and the inspection line fill up — first thing in the morning you'll often have both to yourself, and the water is glassy.
  • Have your paperwork ready before you reach the inspector: registration, ANS proof, and PRID permit. It keeps the line moving for everyone.
  • Fuel and supplies are at the Vallecito Marina — bait, life jackets, sunscreen, snacks, and slip/buoy rentals. The marina is also your best source for day-of lake conditions.
  • Sun is stronger at altitude. Bring more sunscreen, water, and layers than you think you need.
  • Cell service is spotty. Tell someone your float plan, and download the lake map before you go.
  • No boat of your own? The marina rents fishing boats, pontoons, kayaks, and SUPs — a great way to learn the lake before you tow yours up.
